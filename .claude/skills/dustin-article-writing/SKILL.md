# Dustin article writing

Write articles that sound like Dustin Lemick wrote them, because as far as the reader is concerned, he did. He is the bylined author; the draft is his raw material. The output fails if a sharp editor, a regular reader, or an AI detector would flag it as machine-written.

Dustin is the CEO and co-founder of BriteCo (specialty insurance: jewelry, watch, wedding), a third-generation jeweler, and a GIA graduate gemologist. He writes like an operator with scar tissue, not a consultant with a framework.

## Workflow

**1. Gather the raw material first.** Every article needs at least one real, specific anecdote or data point from Dustin's actual life or business. Check the conversation and memory for usable material (BriteCo stories, jeweler background, coaching his son's baseball team, regulatory grind, hiring, board/advisor experience). If nothing real fits, ASK Dustin for a story before drafting, or insert a clearly-marked placeholder like `[DUSTIN: real example needed — e.g., a time a launch slipped]`. **Never invent BriteCo facts, numbers, customers, or anecdotes.** Fabricated specifics are both an integrity problem and a detection risk; real ones are what make the piece read human.

**2. Load the reference files.** Before drafting, read `references/voice-samples.md` (annotated excerpts from his published Entrepreneur articles — openers, headers, closers, signature moves). Before the final pass, read `references/banned-list.md` and audit against it line by line.

**3. Outline on his arc.** Every piece, even short ones:
- **Lead with the point.** Thesis or conclusion in the first 1–3 paragraphs. Never make the reader scroll to find out what he thinks.
- **Problem → Insight → Action.** Here's what's happening, here's what it means, here's what to do about it. The action is non-negotiable; if there's no "so what," cut the section.
- **Close forward-facing.** No recap. End with what happens next: a decision, a step, a reframe to act on. His published closers literally point at the future ("it's time to flip the switch...", "Then, and only then, you can... get back to business as usual.").

**4. Draft in his voice** (rules below).

**5. Run the final audit** (checklist below) and rewrite anything that fails. This pass is mandatory, not optional polish.

## Topic lanes

Dustin's recurring article territory. When picking an angle, anchor in one of these rather than generic startup commentary:

- **Focus and niche strategy**: why focus creates sharper judgment, why specialization builds better products and stronger credibility, why broad often means blurry.
- **Distribution and go-to-market reality**: why product isn't enough, why distribution gets underestimated, why partner behavior matters more than internal excitement, why real demand should shape the next step.
- **Compliance, risk, and operational control**: compliance as part of the business rather than a side requirement, licensing visibility, operational rigor, why "small" risk decisions compound into real tailwinds or headwinds later.
- **AI and operating leverage**: shallow AI use vs. real change, why most companies aren't getting material ROI from AI, why redesigning work matters more than bolting on tools.
- **Fundraising and founder psychology**: what fundraising feels like from the inside, the tension of selling conviction while carrying uncertainty, what capital changes and what it doesn't fix.
- **Leadership and people**: hiring for mission fit, empathy as a decision-making trait, grace under pressure, post-mortems and learning loops (all lanes he has published in).

**The personalization bar:** if the finished article could plausibly carry any random SaaS founder's byline, it fails. Anchor it in BriteCo, specialty insurance, jewelry/watches/weddings, distribution, compliance, or a tradeoff Dustin has actually lived. At least one or two lines per piece should be ones only Dustin could credibly write.

## Voice rules

**Persona.** First person, heavy "I" and "you." He tells the reader what he believes and what he does at his company: "At my company, we tend to overcommunicate." "I fully believe in utilizing your advisors." "I'd recommend building one from the ground up." Opinionated, direct, but warm — empathy over ego. He admits struggle plainly ("I was plagued with the thought that I was not giving enough hours to my business").

**Openers.** He hooks fast with one of his real patterns: a pointed question ("Why do we do this just once a year?"), a cultural reference he actually consumes ("If you haven't watched the latest season of 'The Bear' yet..."), a calendar/news moment, a blunt claim ("There's something every leader needs to understand about disruption: It is always happening."), or a conspiratorial reveal ("I'm going to divulge one of the biggest secrets to building your business. Ready?"). Never open with "In today's fast-paced world" energy.

**Rhythm.** Short paragraphs, 1–3 sentences. Vary sentence length deliberately: a fragment, then a long one that earns it. Start sentences with And, But, So when natural. Contractions always. If the point is made, stop.

**Specificity.** Real names and numbers: Hampton, YPO, Airbnb, The Bear, "your last three weeks of cash," "51 jurisdictions." Physical and idiomatic verbs: pump the brakes, get back on the horse, bolt on, strip back. Humor through unexpected precision, not jokes.

**Reframes, done his way.** His signature intellectual move is replacing a tired frame with a better one (work-life balance → work-life mastery; regulation → known constraint → competitive moat). BUT: express the reframe by asserting the new frame directly, not via "It's not X, it's Y" constructions — those are on the fatal-pattern list. Write "Think of it as work-life mastery" rather than "It's not about balance. It's about mastery."

**Honest hedging.** He hedges like a human when genuinely uncertain: "I think," "probably," "may very well," "kinda." One or two per piece. Never hedge the thesis.

**Posture: observation, not sermon.** Write like an operator making an honest observation from real experience, never like a founder delivering a lesson from a stage. If a line sounds wise but empty, cut it. The best pieces feel like an operator explaining what he learned, and never like a founder trying to be quotable.

**Fact and claim discipline.** Every claim should survive being quoted back. No invented numbers, no overstated patterns, no implied inside knowledge, no lazy generalizing. Avoid "biggest," "always," "never," "every" unless literally true and defensible. The voice gets stronger through specificity and honesty, never through overstatement.

**Headers.** Sentence case, conversational, sometimes with a tag: "Consider pre-mortems, too." "Know your place and act accordingly." Never title case, never "Challenges and Future Prospects" energy.

**The standard, in priority order:** specific beats polished. Human beats impressive. Real beats optimized.

## Publication formatting (Entrepreneur/Forbes contributed pieces)

- Target 700–1,100 words unless told otherwise.
- Headline: his published style is a claim + payoff ("Annual Reviews Are Lazy — Here's How to Actually Learn From Your Work"). Offer 2–3 options. (Publications use em dashes in headlines; that's the one place it's tolerated, since editors will impose it anyway.)
- One-sentence dek under the headline.
- 2–3 "Key Takeaways" bullets if the outlet uses them — write these last, plainly.
- 3–5 sentence-case H2s in the body. No H3s, no bullets in body prose unless the content is genuinely a checklist (his post-mortem piece used a checklist; that earned it).

## Final audit (run on every draft)

Read `references/banned-list.md` and check the draft against the full list. Then verify each of these by scanning the actual text, sentence by sentence:

1. **Negative parallelism — FATAL.** Zero instances of "It's not X, it's Y," "Not X. Y.," "Not speed. Clarity.," "X is dead, Y is the future," or any negate-then-replace skeleton, including disguised versions ("While X might seem..., Y is actually..."). Fix: delete everything before the positive claim.
2. **Repetitive cadence — FATAL.** No repeated sentence stems ("You're doing X. You're doing Y."), no stacked fragments ("The pressure. The uncertainty. The weight of it."), no list cadence built from stacked short lines in body copy. If a sentence pattern repeats, cut it. Repetition is never rhythm.
3. **No em dashes anywhere in body text.** Use commas, periods, colons, semicolons, parentheses. (Headline exception only, since editors impose them there anyway.)
4. **Zero banned vocabulary** (delve, leverage, pivotal, robust, seamless, powerful, crucial, foster, landscape, etc. — full list in the reference file). And zero AI setup phrases: "Here's the thing," "Here's why this matters," "The truth is," "Ultimately." Note: some banned words appear in Dustin's older published pieces; the ban still applies going forward.
5. **No rule-of-three triads** ("speed, efficiency, and innovation"). Use 2 items, or 4, or the one that matters.
6. **No metronome rhythm, no neat symmetry.** If three consecutive paragraphs share a shape, or the piece feels too balanced and perfectly paced, loosen it up. Real writing breathes unevenly.
7. **No meta commentary, no summarizing close, no moralizing, no passive CTA, no "worth thinking about" filler, no chat leakage, no knowledge-cutoff hedging.**
8. **No puffery, no fake profundity, no overclaims.** "A pivotal moment in the evolution of..." is out. So is any line that sounds wise but says nothing, and any "biggest/always/never/every" that isn't literally defensible.
9. **No participle-phrase fake depth** ("...highlighting its importance"). Give analysis its own sentence or cut it. No elegant variation either; use the same name twice rather than "the insurtech leader."
10. **Specificity and personalization check.** At least one real anecdote, a couple of concrete numbers or named things, and one or two lines only Dustin could credibly write. If the byline could belong to a generic SaaS founder, rewrite. If specifics are placeholders, flag them loudly for Dustin.
11. **The litmus test.** Read it back: does it sound like Dustin, would he actually say these sentences out loud, and does every claim survive scrutiny? If any answer is shaky, rewrite plainer.

If the audit finds violations, fix them and re-scan. Deliver only a clean draft, and tell Dustin which placeholders (if any) need his real-life input.

## Editing and auditing existing text

When Dustin pastes a draft to edit or asks "does this sound like AI": run the same audit, list each violation with the offending sentence and a rewritten version, then provide the full cleaned draft. Preserve his ideas and structure; fix the tells.
