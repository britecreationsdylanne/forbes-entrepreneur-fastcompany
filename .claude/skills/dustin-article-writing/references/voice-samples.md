# Voice samples from Dustin's published Entrepreneur articles

Annotated excerpts from his real bylined pieces. Use these to calibrate tone, openers, headers, and closers. Imitate the moves, never copy the sentences.

Caveat: a few of these excerpts contain things the banned list now prohibits (em dashes, "leverage," "It should go without saying"). Those crept in via editors or predate the Voice DNA. Emulate the persona and structure here; enforce the banned list on top.

## Openers (how he hooks)

**The pointed question after a familiar scene** (Annual Reviews Are Lazy, Nov 2025):
> "It's that time of year again — time for the holiday parties, the client gifts and the annual post-mortems... Every time this comes around again on the calendar, I always have one question: Why do we do this just once a year?"
Move: ground in a shared moment, then jab with a question that reframes it.

**The cultural reference he actually consumes** (Grace Is a Leadership Strategy, Aug 2025):
> "If you haven't watched the latest season of 'The Bear' yet, I highly recommend it to any small business owner."
Move: open with TV, sports, or pop culture, then pull a business lesson out of it. He coaches youth baseball and watches what everyone watches; references should be mainstream, not obscure.

**The conspiratorial reveal** (Why Your Business Won't Scale Without Expert Advisors, Jul 2025):
> "I'm going to divulge one of the biggest secrets to building your business. Ready? The more you know, the more you'll grow. That's it. While it may sound trite, the truth is that knowledge is power..."
Move: setup, "Ready?", deliberately simple payoff, then immediately defend the simplicity.

**The blunt claim, then expansion** (Why Most Founders Are Wrong About Disruption, Feb 2024):
> "There's something that every leader needs to understand about disruption: It is always happening."
Move: one-line thesis with a colon. No warm-up.

**The skeptical "what if"** (Work-Life Balance Is a Myth, Sep 2025):
> "But what if none of it is real? What if everything we've been led to believe about work-life balance is just a myth...?"
Move: survey the conventional wisdom in 1–2 sentences, then question all of it.

## Personal vulnerability (he admits the struggle, specifically)

> "I remember feeling this way when I first started my company, BriteCo. I was plagued with the thought that I was not giving enough hours to my business or that I was not committed enough as a father and husband when I was at home."

> "If you've ever had a startup with very narrow margins, you know the immense pressure I'm talking about. Maybe you were on your last three weeks of cash, or lost your most significant partnership and the company went on life support."

Move: name the feeling, anchor it in a concrete moment, never wallow. Two or three sentences max, then back to the lesson.

## Lived specifics (the texture that makes it his)

> "When I go home every night, I sit down and have dinner with my family. I'll still throw the baseball with my son and make time to coach his baseball team."

> "A great newer option I've found is Hampton, a private network for high-growth founders. Of course, there's also YPO..."

> "My industry, insurance, is one of the most heavily regulated, so this has become a real balance we've had to contend with."

> "At my company, we tend to overcommunicate, which I believe is a benefit in the long run."

Move: real product names, real routines, real industry pain. One per section beats five generic claims.

## His reframes (the signature intellectual move)

- "work-life balance" → "work-life mastery"
- Regulation → "a known constraint, and known constraints are much easier to manage than unknown ones" → "regulation can become a superpower. It forces discipline and rewards organizations that invest in compliance."
- Post-mortems → pre-mortems ("the same questions and checklists... thought of through a preliminary perspective")
- "I like to lead with the idea that it's okay to fail, but it's never okay not to try."

When writing new reframes, assert the new frame directly. Do not use "It's not X, it's Y" scaffolding (fatal pattern).

## Headers (sentence case, conversational)

- "It starts with positive self-talk & positive behaviors"
- "Consider pre-mortems, too"
- "Know your place and act accordingly"
- "Understanding regulation can give you a leg up on competition"
- "Making work meaningful"
- "The best ways to utilize your inner circle"

## Idioms and physical language he reaches for

pump the brakes, get back on the horse, road bumps, the other side of the coin, long tentacles in the industry, life support, leg up, sounding board, filling in blind spots, doling out grace, flip the switch, on call, throw the baseball.

Use sparingly: 2–4 per article, where they land naturally.

## Closers (forward-facing, no recap)

> "...it's time to flip the switch and invest in meaningful work that can energize your entire life."

> "Then, and only then, you can find an appropriate reaction and get back to business as usual."

> "In the end, the goal is always about having the best learning opportunities and opening up the lines of communication so all of that knowledge can be applied in the future — whether it's next week or next year."

> "...most importantly, help you navigate and succeed despite whatever change comes."

Move: the last sentence points at action or the future. Never "In conclusion," never a summary of the sections above.

## Hedging, his way

> "I'd also go as far as to say that..."
> "They may very well have their own agenda..."
> "While it may sound trite..."
> "I think of it as filling in blind spots..."

He commits on the thesis and hedges on the particulars. Reverse that and it stops sounding like him.

## Typical shape of a published piece

1. Hook (question / culture / blunt claim), 1–3 short paragraphs
2. Personal stake: a BriteCo or founder moment
3. 3–5 sentence-case H2 sections, each one Problem → Insight → Action
4. Concrete tools, names, or practices in at least one section
5. Forward-facing closer, one or two sentences

Word count 700–1,100. Paragraphs 1–4 sentences. One rhetorical question per piece is plenty; two max.
