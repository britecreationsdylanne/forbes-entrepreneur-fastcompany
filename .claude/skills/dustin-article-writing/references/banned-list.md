# Banned list and AI-pattern audit reference

Source of truth: Dustin's Voice DNA. If even ONE hard-rule item appears in a final draft, the output fails. Audit every draft against this file before delivery.

## 1. FATAL: negative parallelisms and reframe constructions

The single most reliable tell of AI text. Zero tolerance. If found, rewrite the entire sentence by deleting everything before the positive claim.

Banned patterns:
- "This isn't X. This is Y."
- "Not X. Y."
- "Forget X. This is Y."
- "Less X, more Y."
- "Not only X, but also Y."
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y."
- "No X, no Y, just Z."
- "X? No. Y."
- "Stop thinking X. Start thinking Y."
- "It's not about X. It's about Y."
- "X is dead. Y is the future."
- "The question isn't X. The question is Y."
- "You don't need X. You need Y."
- "X is overrated. Y is what matters."
- ANY sentence that negates one framing then asserts a corrected one.

Disguised versions (equally banned):
- "While X might seem right, Y is actually..."
- "Sure, X works. But Y is where the real..."
- "X gets all the attention, but Y is what actually..."

The fix: if the draft says "It's not about the prompt. It's about the context," write "It's about the context."

## 1B. FATAL: repetitive cadence and stacked fragments

Repetition is never rhythm. If a sentence pattern repeats, cut it.

Banned:
- Repeated sentence stems: "You're doing X. You're doing Y. You're doing Z." / "It helps with X. It helps with Y." / "A company can do X. A company can do Y."
- Repeated openings: "when it..." stacks, "it takes..." stacks, "are we..." question stems.
- Stacked fragment piles for emphasis: "Not charisma. Not big moments. Not the occasional great call." / "The pressure. The uncertainty. The weight of it."
- List cadence inside body copy (fake rhythm through stacked short lines): "You hire too fast. You expand too fast. You add complexity too fast."

A single intentional fragment for pacing is fine. A pile of them is a tell.

## 1C. Hard bans on posture

- **No neat symmetry.** If the piece feels too balanced, too polished, too perfectly paced, loosen it up.
- **No fake profundity.** If a line sounds wise but empty, cut it.
- **No sermon voice.** Never write like Dustin is delivering a lesson from a stage. Write like he is making an honest observation from real experience.
- **No overclaims.** Avoid "biggest," "always," "never," "every" unless literally true and defensible.
- **No AI setup phrases:** "Here's the thing," "Here's why this matters," "The truth is."

## 2. Dead AI vocabulary (never use)

delve, realm, harness, unlock, tapestry, paradigm, cutting-edge, revolutionize, landscape (abstract), intricate/intricacies, showcasing, crucial, pivotal, surpass, meticulously, vibrant, unparalleled, underscore (verb), leverage, synergy, innovative, game-changer, testament, commendable, meticulous, highlight (verb), emphasize, boast, groundbreaking, align, foster, showcase, enhance, holistic, garner, accentuate, pioneering, trailblazing, unleash, versatile, transformative, redefine, seamless, optimize, scalable, robust, breakthrough, empower, streamline, frictionless, elevate, adaptive, effortless, data-driven, insightful, proactive, mission-critical, visionary, disruptive, reimagine, unprecedented, intuitive, leading-edge, synergize, democratize, accelerate, state-of-the-art, dynamic, immersive, predictive, transparent, proprietary, integrated, plug-and-play, turnkey, future-proof, paradigm-shifting, supercharge, enduring, interplay, valuable, captivate

Also banned: "serves as," "stands as," "marks a," "represents a," "boasts a," "features a," "offers a" when used to dodge "is" or "has." Just say "is." And "powerful" as a hype adjective.

Carve-outs for literal topics: "disruption" as the actual subject of a piece (he has a whole article on it) and "operating leverage" as a literal financial/operational concept are allowed. "Disruptive" as a hype adjective and "leverage" as a verb ("leverage your network") stay banned.

## 3. Dead phrases (never use)

- "In today's [anything]..."
- "It's important to note that..." / "It's worth noting..."
- "In order to" (say "to")
- "I'd be happy to help"
- "Straightforward"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore" / "Let's unpack" / "Delve into"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Moving forward"
- "To put this in perspective..."
- "What makes this particularly interesting is..."
- "The implications here are..."
- "In other words..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "Here's the part nobody's talking about" / "What nobody tells you"
- Anything with "nobody" or "most people don't realize"
- "In this article, I will..." (all meta commentary)
- "Despite its [positives], [subject] faces challenges..."
- "Challenges and Future Prospects" as a header

## 4. Dead transitions (never use)

- "Furthermore" / "Additionally" / "Moreover"
- "That said" / "That being said"
- "Ultimately"
- "With that in mind"
- "It is also worth mentioning"
- "On top of that"
- Any mechanical connector that reads like a college essay

## 5. Engagement bait and hype (never use)

- "Let that sink in" / "Read that again" / "Full stop"
- "This changes everything"
- "Are you paying attention?" / "You're not ready for this"
- "Supercharge" / "Unlock" / "Future-proof" / "10x your [anything]"
- Promises of superpowers, easy riches, or overnight transformation

## 6. Structural AI patterns (scan and fix)

- **Puffery / significance inflation:** "a pivotal moment in the evolution of...", "marking a significant shift toward...", "setting the stage for..." State the fact; let the reader judge.
- **Rule of three:** "speed, efficiency, and innovation." Use 2 items, or 4, or just the one that matters.
- **False ranges:** "from ancient traditions to modern innovations." If there's no meaningful middle ground, delete it.
- **Elegant variation:** person → "the protagonist" → "the key player." Repeat the name instead.
- **Meta commentary:** "In this section we will discuss..." Say the thing.
- **Participle-phrase fake depth:** "...highlighting its importance," "...underscoring its significance," "...reflecting broader trends." Delete the phrase or give the analysis its own sentence.
- **Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers:** "As of my last update..." Never.
- **Chat leakage:** "I hope this helps!", "Would you like me to...", "Great question!" Never in article text.
- **Metronome rhythm:** every sentence medium length, every paragraph 3–4 sentences. Break it: short, then long, then a fragment.
- **Copulative avoidance:** "serves as" / "holds the distinction of being" → "is."
- **Title-case headers:** use sentence case.
- **Em dashes:** banned in body text (commas, periods, colons, semicolons, parentheses instead). Headline-only exception.
- **Bad endings:** no recap of the whole article, no moralizing, no passive CTA, no "worth thinking about" filler. Close forward-facing with a clear point.

## 7. Frequency guidance (anti-overfitting)

- HARD RULES (sections 1–5, em dashes, title case): absolute, never violate.
- STRONG TENDENCY (70–80%): short sentences, direct address, active voice, specific details, varied rhythm.
- LIGHT PREFERENCE: specific word choices, structure choices, humor placement.

Don't use the same opening formula every time. Let the content dictate structure. Litmus test: "Does this sound like something Dustin would actually write, or like an AI trying very hard to imitate him?" If it feels forced, pull back.
